How to Connect Google Search Console to Your Website

Google Search Console is a free tool that shows you how Google sees your site — what keywords you rank for, how many impressions you get, and any crawl issues. Here's how to set it up from scratch.

What is Google Search Console?

Google Search Console (GSC) lets you monitor your website's presence in Google Search results. Once connected, you can see:

  • Which keywords bring people to your site — and your average ranking position for each
  • Impressions and clicks — how often your pages appear in search and how many people click
  • Index coverage — which pages Google has indexed and which have errors
  • Core Web Vitals — page experience signals that affect your rankings
  • Manual actions and security issues — Google's way of flagging serious problems
💡
Free forever. Google Search Console has no paid tier — all data is free. You just need a Google account and to prove you own the site.

Step-by-Step Setup

1

Go to Google Search Console

Visit search.google.com/search-console and sign in with your Google account. Use the same account you use for Google Analytics if you have it.

2

Add a Property

Click "Add property" in the top left. You'll see two options — choose the one that fits your setup:

Option A — Domain property (recommended)

Covers your entire domain including all subdomains (www, blog, etc.) and both HTTP and HTTPS. Enter just your domain name, e.g. lighthouselaunch.com.

This requires adding a DNS TXT record to verify ownership — your domain registrar (GoDaddy, Namecheap, etc.) is where you do this.

Option B — URL prefix property

Covers only one specific URL, e.g. https://www.lighthouselaunch.com. Easier to verify but you may need separate properties for subdomains.

ℹ️
Which should you choose? If you have one simple website, Domain property is better — it captures everything in one place. If you're running multiple subdomains you want to track separately, use URL prefix.
3

Verify Ownership

Google needs to confirm you own the site before giving you access to its data. There are several methods depending on your property type.

Method 1 — DNS record (for Domain properties)

  1. Google will show you a TXT record like: google-site-verification=abc123xyz...
  2. Log in to your DNS provider (GoDaddy, Cloudflare, etc.)
  3. Add a new TXT record with the Name @ and the value Google gave you
  4. Click Verify in Search Console — DNS can take a few minutes to propagate

Method 2 — HTML tag (easiest for URL prefix)

  1. Google gives you a <meta> tag like:
    <meta name="google-site-verification" content="abc123" />
  2. Paste it inside the <head> section of your homepage
  3. Deploy the change, then click Verify

Method 3 — Google Analytics (if GA4 is already installed)

If you already have Google Analytics on your site with the same Google account, Search Console can use that to verify automatically — just select this option and click Verify.

4

Submit Your Sitemap

A sitemap tells Google about all the pages on your site so it can crawl them faster. Once verified, go to Sitemaps in the left menu and submit your sitemap URL.

Most websites have a sitemap at one of these URLs — try them in your browser first:

  • https://yoursite.com/sitemap.xml
  • https://yoursite.com/sitemap_index.xml
  • https://yoursite.com/robots.txt — check here for the sitemap location
⚠️
No sitemap? If your site doesn't have one, most CMS platforms (WordPress, Webflow, Squarespace, Shopify) generate them automatically. For custom sites, tools like xml-sitemaps.com can generate one for free.
5

Wait for Data

Search Console shows data from the last 16 months, but it takes 2–3 days for the first data to appear after connecting. Some reports take up to 28 days to populate fully.

What to Check Once It's Set Up

  • Performance — Your top queries, pages, countries, and devices. This is the most important report.
  • Coverage — Pages Google has indexed vs. excluded. Fix any errors here first.
  • Core Web Vitals — Page experience signals. Aim for all URLs in the "Good" bucket.
  • Links — Which external sites link to you and your most-linked pages.
  • Mobile Usability — Any pages with mobile UX issues that could hurt rankings.
